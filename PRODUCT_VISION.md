# Product Operating Software — Vision & Strategy

> **Status:** Draft 1.0  
> **Purpose:** Foundational context for all product and engineering decisions

---

## The Problem

Product leadership is fundamentally a coaching discipline — but the infrastructure for coaching is borrowed from tools built for everything else.

**Current state:** 1:1 agendas in FigJam. Notes in Outlook and Confluence. Structured feedback reserved for annual reviews. No coherent system connecting skill expectations, development goals, and coaching conversations.

### Symptoms

| Symptom | Description |
|---|---|
| **Fragmented Signal** | Coaching artifacts live across four different tools. A PM's growth narrative exists nowhere as a coherent whole. |
| **Episodic Feedback** | Structured feedback is reserved for annual reviews — by then, patterns are entrenched and opportunities are lost. |
| **Implicit Expectations** | PMs rarely know what "great" looks like at their level or the next. Without a shared skills framework, effort gets misdirected. |

### Consequences

- **Stalled careers** — PMs who could be exceptional remain average, not from lack of talent but lack of a clear development loop
- **Poor products** — Product quality compounds on PM capability; skill gaps are fixable with the right infrastructure
- **Leader burnout** — Product Leaders who care exhaust themselves holding all this context manually

---

## Product Vision

> **Product Operating Software is the operating layer for product coaching** — the place where skill expectations are made visible, development is continuous rather than episodic, and every Product Leader can build the kind of team that ships great work reliably.

We believe the best product cultures aren't born — they're built. Building them requires the same rigor product leaders apply to their roadmaps: clarity on outcomes, structured processes, and tight feedback loops.

## Strategic Pillars

### P.01 — Shared Language: A Living Skills Framework, Not a Static Rubric

The foundation of great coaching is shared vocabulary. Product Operating Software provides a structured, opinionated PM skills taxonomy — organized by domain (discovery, execution, strategy, influence, craft) and level — that teams can adopt, adapt, and evolve.

The framework isn't a compliance artifact. It's the living contract between a PM and their leader about what excellence looks like right now and at the next level.

**Key capabilities:**
- Skills taxonomy organized by domain and level
- Level-based competency bands
- Customizable per org
- Benchmark library

---

### P.02 — Continuous Signal: Assessments That Feed Coaching, Not Just Reviews

Structured skill assessments should happen continuously — not annually. The platform enables lightweight, recurring assessments that capture both leader observations and PM self-reflection.

Over time, these build a longitudinal performance narrative: where a PM started, where they've grown, where current gaps are, and what needs focus. This is the record that makes 1:1s strategic instead of reactive.

**Key capabilities:**
- Recurring skill check-ins
- Self + manager assessment
- Trend visualization over time
- Gap identification

---

### P.03 — Structured Development: Goals and Growth Plans That Actually Stick

Development goals live and die by accountability infrastructure. Product Operating Software connects skill gaps directly to time-bound goals, recommended growth actions, and upskill resources — so the path from "here's the gap" to "here's what we're doing about it" is never ambiguous.

Growth plans are co-created, tracked, and revisited — not filed away after the conversation ends.

**Key capabilities:**
- Gap-to-goal linking
- Development action library
- Progress tracking
- Resource recommendations

---

### P.04 — Coaching Infrastructure: 1:1s and Coaching Sessions With Memory

The 1:1 is the primary coaching vehicle — and it has no memory. Product Operating Software gives every 1:1 a structured home: agenda templates informed by current development priorities, session notes that auto-surface relevant context, and action items that carry forward.

Leaders stop starting from scratch each week. PMs stop wondering if the last conversation mattered.

**Key capabilities:**
- Structured 1:1 agendas
- Contextual note-taking
- Action item continuity across sessions
- Coaching moment tagging

---

### P.05 — Team Intelligence: Portfolio View for Leaders Managing Multiple PMs

A Director or VP managing 4–8 PMs needs a coherent view of the team's capability landscape — where collective strength lies, where team-wide gaps threaten delivery, who is ready to stretch, and where attrition risk may be building.

Product Operating Software gives leaders a team-level intelligence layer that makes coaching conversations with their own leaders more credible, and hiring decisions more intentional.

**Key capabilities:**
- Team skill heatmap
- Readiness signals
- Coaching time allocation visibility
- Succession indicators

## Users

### Primary: The Product Leader
Director or VP of Product at a growth-stage or enterprise company. Manages 3–8 PMs. Deeply invested in developing their team but currently holds too much context in their head and not enough in systems.

**Core needs:**
- Coaching that is continuous, not episodic
- Shared language for PM performance expectations
- Ability to tell a team capability story to their own leadership
- Coaching context that persists when PMs change or teams reorganize
- 1:1s that are strategic, not reactive

### Secondary: The Product Manager
Mid to senior PM at a company with a defined product function. Ambitious and growth-oriented, but navigating unclear expectations and infrequent structured feedback.

**Core needs:**
- Clarity on what "great" looks like at their level and the next
- Feedback specific enough to act on
- A visible record of their own growth trajectory
- Development conversations grounded in data, not impressions
- Agency over their growth plan — not just a recipient of it

## Design Principles

### 1. Coaching-First, Not HR-First
This is a development tool, not a performance management system. Language, design, and defaults should feel like a growth conversation — not a compliance audit.

### 2. Continuous Over Episodic
Every architectural decision should move coaching from annual events to weekly habits. Low-friction inputs. Short feedback loops. Momentum over ceremony.

### 3. Context That Travels
Coaching context should persist across leaders, reorgs, and role changes. The PM's growth story belongs to them — not to their manager's memory.

### 4. Specific Enough to Act
Vague feedback is worse than no feedback. Every assessment, goal, and coaching note should meet a bar of specificity that produces a concrete next action.

### 5. Built for Trust, Not Surveillance
PMs must feel safe being honest in self-assessments. Visibility controls and tone matter enormously. The platform must earn trust before it earns adoption.

### 6. Opinionated by Default, Flexible by Design
Ship with a strong default skills framework and process model. Let teams diverge where needed — but never require them to build from scratch to get started.

## Open Strategic Questions

1. **Buy-in sequencing** — Leaders adopt it, but PMs have to trust it. The PM experience may need to feel more like a personal career record than a manager's tool.
2. **Integration vs. replacement** — Do we live alongside existing tools (Confluence, Slack, JIRA) or eventually replace the 1:1 artifact entirely? This is a positioning and pricing decision.
3. **The wedge** — Skills framework + assessment loop is the natural land-and-expand entry point. Creates immediate value without requiring full workflow adoption.
